Brasília, September 12, 2024

More than 2.3 million Brazilians have hearing impairment, and the Brazilian Sign Language (Libras) is essential to enable communication. The daily problem is the opposite: most of the population that does not master Libras hits blockers whenever they meet this community. **This structural friction is what the undergraduate thesis defended in September 2024 by Daniela Soares de Oliveira and Danilo Domingo Vitoriano Silva addresses with machine learning**, under the advising of Prof. {{< link-interno "/people/george_marsicano" "George Marsicano Corrêa" >}}.

The work develops an AI model for automatic translation of Libras, tackling a classic computer-vision-for-sign-language problem: distinguishing similar gestures, sustaining accuracy under variable lighting and framing, and responding in time to enable dialogue. The approach ties {{< link-interno "/areas/ai_pln/" "machine learning" >}} to a directly social problem — the kind of work the academic community must sustain, because the market alone will not push it.

The result is input for developers willing to build inclusive applications — in public services, in customer support, in classrooms. A model, on its own, does not change the world; but the right model, placed in the right hands, changes who enters a dialogue and who is left outside.

The full text — written in Portuguese — is available at the [UnB Undergraduate Theses Digital Library](https://www.bdm.unb.br/handle/10483/43572).
