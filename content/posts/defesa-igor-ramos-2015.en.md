Brasília, 2015

Any researcher who has tried to consolidate hundreds of papers into a usable synthesis knows where things get stuck: turning the collected material into a map that others can actually navigate. **This was the bottleneck Igor de Araújo Ramos chose to face in his undergraduate thesis defended in 2015 at FCTE/UnB**, advised by Prof. {{< link-interno "/people/fabiana_mendes" "Fabiana Freitas Mendes" >}}.

The work departs from a diagnosis shared across the Evidence-Based Software Engineering community: systematic mapping is now one of the most widely adopted methodologies for structured reviews — and yet the very step of building the representative knowledge map suffers from a lack of clear process and supporting tools. The result is well known: hand-crafted maps built with hard-to-reproduce criteria and poor comparability across studies.

The author’s proposal places ontologies at the backbone of that step. More than a taxonomy of concepts, an ontology works as a shared vocabulary that captures the relationships found in the reviewed papers — and from that, the map can be generated systematically. The methodological move speaks both to {{< link-interno "/areas/ai_pln/" "AI and knowledge representation" >}} and to practices of {{< link-interno "/areas/transformation/" "digital transformation" >}} of scientific work itself: more traceability, less improvisation.

The full text — written in Portuguese — is available at the [UnB Undergraduate Theses Digital Library](https://www.bdm.unb.br/handle/10483/11334).
